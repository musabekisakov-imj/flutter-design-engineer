# Visual Direction

## Contents

- Direction statement
- Product fit
- System decisions
- Comparison method

## Direction statement

Before drawing widgets, write one sentence that connects visual character to product behavior. Example: “A calm, precise scheduling interface with editorial hierarchy, compact controls, warm neutral surfaces, and motion limited to continuity and confirmation.”

Avoid adjectives without operational consequences. “Modern and clean” does not determine type scale, density, geometry, palette, imagery, or motion.

## Product fit

Answer:

- Who acts, under what conditions, and how often?
- What must be noticed first and what can recede?
- Is the interface exploratory, transactional, operational, expressive, or safety-critical?
- Which platform conventions create trust or speed?
- Which existing brand or product cues must remain?

## System decisions

Specify:

- typography character and hierarchy;
- compact, balanced, or spacious density;
- neutral and accent behavior in light/dark themes;
- sharp, soft, or mixed geometry;
- flat, bordered, tonal, or elevated surface strategy;
- icon and imagery role;
- direct, calm, playful, or cinematic motion character.

Make one strong choice per dimension. Exceptions need product reasons.

## Comparison method

When direction is open, offer at most three coherent approaches. Describe the same representative screen in each, name the trade-off, recommend one, and obtain approval before broad implementation.
